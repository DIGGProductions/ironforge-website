A preventive maintenance schedule is the difference between deciding when your truck is in the shop and having that decided for you on the side of the road. The trucks that break down and the trucks that fail inspections are almost always the ones nobody put on a schedule. Here is how to build one that fits the work.

## Miles, hours, or time, whichever comes first

A truck that runs the highway racks up miles. A truck that idles a job site racks up hours without many miles. A truck that sits half the year still needs service on a calendar. A good schedule triggers on whichever comes first, so a low-mileage truck does not skip a year and a hard-running one does not stretch an interval it cannot afford.

## What belongs on a PM

A real PM is more than an oil change. Oil and filters, fuel and air filters, and the fluids people forget until they fail. A look at the [brakes](/services/brake-repair), the [steering and suspension](/services/steering-suspension), the [tires](/services/tires), and the lights. Belts, hoses, and batteries, the wear items that strand a truck. And a check for leaks before they become a puddle and a tow.

## Tier it so it makes sense

Most shops run PM in tiers. A short-interval service covers oil, filters, and a safety look-over. A longer-interval service adds the bigger items like fuel filters, differential and transmission fluid, and a deeper inspection. You do not do everything every visit, you do the right things at the right interval, which keeps the cost sane and the truck covered.

A schedule you do not track is not a schedule. The trucks that lapse are the ones where the date lived in somebody's head.
